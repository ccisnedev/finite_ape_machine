# Peirce's Three Modes of Inference

## Abduction — Hypothesis Generation

Abduction is the logic of discovery. Peirce defines it as the process of forming explanatory hypotheses from surprising observations:

> "The surprising fact C is observed; but if A were true, C would be a matter of course; hence, there is reason to suspect that A is true." (CP 5.189)

Abduction is the **only** logical operation that introduces new ideas:

> "Abduction is the process of forming an explanatory hypothesis. It is the only logical operation which introduces any new idea." (CP 5.171)

It does not prove — it suggests. Its conclusion is merely a conjecture worthy of testing.

## Deduction — Consequence Derivation

Deduction draws necessary consequences from a hypothesis. Given premises, it yields conclusions that **must** follow:

> "Deduction is the only reasoning which is strictly demonstrative... the only reasoning that can be made exact." (CP 2.267)

Deduction takes the hypothesis generated by abduction and asks: "If this hypothesis is true, what observable consequences follow?" It produces testable predictions but adds no new empirical content.

## Induction — Empirical Testing

Induction is the testing of deductive predictions against reality:

> "Induction is the experimental testing of a theory." (CP 6.526)

It classifies into three kinds (CP 2.755–2.760): crude induction (no counterexamples found), qualitative induction (weight of evidence), and quantitative induction (statistical sampling). Its function is to evaluate the hypothesis that abduction proposed.

## The Cyclic Unity

Peirce insists that the three modes form an inseparable whole:

> "These three kinds of reasoning... constitute an irreducible triad... They cannot be reduced to one another or to any simpler forms." (CP 2.775)

The cycle operates as: Abduction generates a hypothesis → Deduction derives its consequences → Induction tests those consequences. If induction fails, abduction generates a new hypothesis and the cycle repeats.

## Self-Correcting Nature

Peirce's inquiry is inherently self-correcting. Failed inductions do not terminate inquiry — they generate new surprising facts that trigger fresh abduction:

> "The irritation of doubt causes a struggle to attain a state of belief. I shall term this struggle Inquiry." (CP 5.374)

Doubt is not a defect but the engine of inquiry. The fixation of belief (CP 5.377) occurs only when all three modes converge on a stable conclusion.

## Key Properties

- **Abduction**: ampliative, fallible, creative — the source of novelty
- **Deduction**: explicative, certain, mechanical — the source of rigor
- **Induction**: ampliative, self-correcting, empirical — the source of validation
- **Together**: necessary and sufficient for any complete act of inquiry (CP 7.218)

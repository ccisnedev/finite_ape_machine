# Dewey's Theory of Inquiry

## The Canonical Definition

From *Logic: The Theory of Inquiry* (1938, p.108):

> "Inquiry is the controlled or directed transformation of an indeterminate situation into one that is so determinate in its constituent distinctions and relations as to convert the elements of the original situation into a unified whole."

### Component Analysis

| Component | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Controlled or directed** | Inquiry is not random — it follows a method |
| **Transformation** | The situation itself changes, not just our beliefs about it |
| **Indeterminate situation** | A situation that is unsettled, doubtful, problematic |
| **Determinate** | Clear, resolved, with distinctions articulated |
| **Constituent distinctions and relations** | The parts and their connections become visible |
| **Unified whole** | Resolution integrates all elements coherently |

## The Five Phases of Reflective Thought

From *How We Think* (1910, Ch.6):

1. **Suggestion** — A felt difficulty; something is "off"
2. **Intellectualization** — Defining the problem precisely; transforming vague difficulty into a specific question
3. **Hypothesis** — Generating possible explanations or solutions (maps to Peirce's abduction)
4. **Reasoning** — Working out implications of the hypothesis (maps to Peirce's deduction)
5. **Testing** — Verifying by action or observation (maps to Peirce's induction)

Dewey emphasizes these are not rigid stages but fluid phases that may overlap or recurse (*How We Think*, 1910, p.72).

## Inquiry vs. Documentation

A crucial distinction: inquiry is an **activity**, not a record. The product of inquiry (knowledge, a solution) is different from the process. However, Dewey notes that articulating the process aids in its self-correction — making reasoning explicit makes it criticizable (*Logic*, 1938, p.116).

Documentation serves inquiry by:
- Making the indeterminate situation explicit (problem statement)
- Recording hypotheses for evaluation
- Preserving reasoning chains for review
- Enabling community participation in the inquiry

## The Social Nature of Inquiry

Dewey (via Shields 2003, "The Community of Inquiry: Classical Pragmatism and Public Administration") insists inquiry is inherently social:

- Knowledge is validated through **communal testing**, not private certainty
- A "community of inquiry" (term from Peirce, developed by Dewey) shares methods, standards, and results
- Individual inquirers have specialized roles but contribute to collective understanding
- The community provides the check against individual bias (Seixas 1993, "The Community of Inquiry as a Basis for Knowledge and Learning")

## Indeterminate → Determinate: The Pattern

Dewey's pattern of inquiry maps to any problem-solving domain:

```
Indeterminate situation → Problem formulation → Hypothesis →
Reasoning about consequences → Experimental action → Resolution
```

The key insight: the **situation itself** is what is indeterminate, not merely our knowledge. Inquiry transforms reality, not just belief. This is Dewey's departure from both rationalism and naive empiricism.
